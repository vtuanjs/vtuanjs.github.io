Years ago I worked on a payment gateway that connected to a growing list of banks. Every transfer had a fee, based on which bank and how much moved — over $100, charge a dollar; over $200, charge two. Each bank had also negotiated its own adjustment on top, and one large customer negotiated a discount on top of that: their transfers through Bank X needed a lower fee than everyone else's. All of that logic lived buried in the engineering codebase as a tangle of exceptions on bank, amount, and now customer.

- Every new bank, every renegotiated rate, every customer deal meant an engineer had to change it.
- No one could say with confidence what the fee would be for a given bank, amount, and customer without tracing every exception by hand.
- Finance and ops — the people actually negotiating the rates with banks and customers — couldn't touch any of it. Every change was a ticket to engineering.

**Business rules that change on a business cadence — fees, eligibility, pricing tiers — should be something the business edits directly, safely, without an engineer and a deploy in the loop.**

This is part 6, the last piece of the series on our identity system. Part 5 covered the config catalog — a value set once per customer for the life of their contract. This part is one step further: not a single value, but a rule, and a set of rules that need to be authored, ordered, and changed by the business itself.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Problem** | Fee schedules and pricing rules live buried in the engineering codebase as a pile of exceptions. Every change to a rate, a tier, a bank's terms, or a customer's negotiated discount requires an engineer to edit, review, and ship it. |
| **Why** | It was written the only way anyone builds this the first time: as a growing pile of case-by-case exceptions. That's fast to build once and unmanageable to keep changing. |
| **Goal** | The people who own the business terms — finance, ops, pricing — change the rules themselves, safely, and the change is live immediately. |

## Why it matters

**A fee schedule buried in engineering logic doesn't just slow down changes — it becomes something no one fully trusts.** Every fix risks breaking something else buried in the same tangle of exceptions. The business impact isn't hypothetical:

- A wrong tier boundary.
- A stale bank rate.
- A customer discount that didn't actually apply.

**Each one is either money given away or a rate never agreed to** — and the people who negotiated the actual numbers can't act on it without a ticket and a wait for the next release.

## Make the rule something the business owns, not code

**Instead of a pile of exceptions buried in code, the fee schedule becomes a set of rules that finance and ops write directly — and every rule is checked before it's allowed to go live, so a business user can change the schedule but can't accidentally break it.**

The fee for a transfer becomes a decision: given the bank, the amount, and who's sending it, what's the fee.

- **Ops writes the rule** by picking the bank, amount threshold, and customer if one applies, then setting the fee — not by writing code.
- **The system checks every rule as it's saved** — real bank, real customer, real amount, does this rule make sense — before it's allowed near a live transaction.
- **A rule that passes goes live immediately.** No deploy, no engineer, no waiting for the next release window.

**Benefit:** the fee schedule updates the moment the business decides it should, with no risk of a malformed rule reaching production, because nothing gets saved until it's been checked.

## How a transfer actually gets evaluated

A transfer comes in, and the rules are checked in order until one matches — the first match wins, and if nothing matches, a default fee applies rather than the transfer failing.

**Order matters as much as the rules themselves.** Acme's discount rule has to sit above the general $200 tier, or the generic rule fires first and Acme never sees the deal it negotiated — a rule catching a case earlier always wins over a more specific one further down. That's not a detail to bury in a settings screen; it has to be visible to whoever is arranging the rules.

## The trade-off

| | Business rule in code | Checked rules, business-owned |
|---|---|---|
| Who can change a rate | An engineer | Finance or ops directly |
| Time to ship a change | A release cycle | Immediate |
| Bad input | Caught in code review, if reviewed carefully | Rejected automatically before it's saved |
| Cost | Cheap to write once | Building the checking layer, once |
| Reach | Whatever rule was hardcoded first — a fee schedule, an eligibility check | Any rule that changes on a business cadence — eligibility, pricing tiers, routing |

**The cost is real:**

- Someone has to build the layer that checks a rule before it's saved.
- Someone has to own what happens when a rule is wrong anyway — a wrong rate is money, so it needs a record of who set it and when, ideally a way to test a rule against a sample transfer before it goes live.

The alternative isn't cheaper. It moves the cost onto engineering time spent on rate tickets, and onto the business waiting on a release train to react to a bank renegotiating its terms. You pay either way — the difference is whether the people who own the number can act on it themselves.

## What's next

The fee schedule was the first place this pattern showed up, but it's not really about fees. Any rule that the business needs to change faster than engineering can ship it — eligibility, pricing tiers, discount thresholds — fits the same shape: rules the business owns, checked before they go live.

That's the series: an org that provisions itself, a person whose access resolves across orgs, a flag that separates merging from releasing, a config catalog for what's permanent per customer, and a rules engine for the logic that changes on a business cadence, not an engineering one.
