Years ago I worked on a payment gateway that connected to a growing list of banks. Every transfer had a fee, and the fee depended on two things: which bank the transfer went through, and how much money moved. Over $100, charge a dollar. Over $200, charge two. Each bank had also negotiated its own adjustment on top — a few cents more here, a discount there. Then a large customer negotiated their own discount on top of all of that — their transfers through Bank X needed a lower fee than everyone else's transfers through Bank X. All of that logic was buried inside the engineering codebase, as a tangle of exceptions layered on bank, amount, and now customer. Every new bank, every renegotiated rate, every customer-specific deal, every new tier meant an engineer had to go in and change it. It grew until no one could look at it and say with confidence what the fee would be for a given bank, amount, and customer without tracing every exception by hand — and the people who actually owned those numbers, finance and ops, the ones sitting across the table from banks and customers negotiating the rates, couldn't touch any of it. Every rate change, every new customer deal, was a ticket to engineering.

**Business rules that change on a business cadence — fees, eligibility, pricing tiers — should be something the business edits directly, safely, without an engineer and a deploy in the loop.**

This is part 6, the last piece of the series on our identity system. Part 5 covered the config catalog — a value set once per customer for the life of their contract. This part is one step further: not a single value, but a rule, and a set of rules that need to be authored, ordered, and changed by the business itself.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Problem** | Fee schedules and pricing rules live buried in the engineering codebase as a pile of exceptions. Every change to a rate, a tier, a bank's terms, or a customer's negotiated discount requires an engineer to edit, review, and ship it. |
| **Why** | It was written the only way anyone builds this the first time: as a growing pile of case-by-case exceptions. That's fast to build once and unmanageable to keep changing. |
| **Goal** | The people who own the business terms — finance, ops, pricing — change the rules themselves, safely, and the change is live immediately. |

## Why it matters

A fee schedule buried in engineering logic doesn't just slow down changes — it becomes something no one fully trusts. Every fix risks breaking something else buried in the same tangle of exceptions, and the business impact isn't hypothetical: a wrong tier boundary, a stale bank rate, or a customer discount that didn't actually apply is either money the company gives away or a rate a bank or customer never agreed to pay. Meanwhile the people who negotiated the actual numbers have no way to act on them without opening a ticket and waiting for a release.

## Make the rule something the business owns, not code

**Instead of a pile of exceptions buried in code, the fee schedule becomes a set of rules that finance and ops write directly — and every rule is checked before it's allowed to go live, so a business user can change the schedule but can't accidentally break it.**

The fee for a transfer becomes a decision: given the bank, the amount, and who's sending it, what's the fee. Ops writes that rule by picking the bank, the amount threshold, the customer if one applies, and setting the fee — not by writing code. The system checks every rule as it's saved: is this a real bank, is this a real customer, is the amount a real number, does this rule actually make sense — before it's allowed anywhere near a live transaction. A rule that passes goes live immediately. No deploy, no engineer, no waiting for the next release window.

**Benefit:** the fee schedule updates the moment the business decides it should, with no risk of a malformed rule reaching production, because nothing gets saved until it's been checked.

## How a transfer actually gets evaluated

A transfer comes in, and the rules are checked in order until one matches — the first match wins, and if nothing matches, a default fee applies rather than the transfer failing.

Order matters as much as the rules themselves. Acme's discount rule has to sit above the general $200 tier, or the generic rule fires first and Acme never sees the deal it negotiated. A rule catching a case earlier always wins over a more specific one further down. That's not a detail to bury in a settings screen; it's part of the decision, and it has to be visible to whoever is arranging the rules.

## The trade-off

| | Business rule in code | Checked rules, business-owned |
|---|---|---|
| Who can change a rate | An engineer | Finance or ops directly |
| Time to ship a change | A release cycle | Immediate |
| Bad input | Caught in code review, if reviewed carefully | Rejected automatically before it's saved |
| Cost | Cheap to write once | Building the checking layer, once |
| Reach | Whatever rule was hardcoded first — a fee schedule, an eligibility check | Any rule that changes on a business cadence — eligibility, pricing tiers, routing |

The cost is real: someone has to build the layer that checks a rule before it's saved, and someone has to own what happens when a rule is wrong anyway — a wrong rate is money, so it needs a record of who set it and when, and ideally a way to try a rule against a sample transfer before it goes live. That's not free. But the alternative isn't actually cheaper — it just moves the cost onto engineering time spent on rate tickets, and onto the business waiting on a release train to react to a bank renegotiating its terms. You pay either way. The difference is whether the people who own the number can act on it themselves.

## What's next

The fee schedule was the first place this pattern showed up, but it's not really about fees. Any rule that the business needs to change faster than engineering can ship it — eligibility, pricing tiers, discount thresholds — fits the same shape: rules the business owns, checked before they go live.

That's the series: an org that provisions itself, a person whose access resolves across orgs, a flag that separates merging from releasing, a config catalog for what's permanent per customer, and a rules engine for the logic that changes on a business cadence, not an engineering one.
