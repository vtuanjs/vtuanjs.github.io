On the same enterprise LMS from the flag post — every customer runs a different operation and wants a feature the others don't, or a threshold set differently for them. Before org config existed, the only way to do that was to check the customer's identity right inside the logic: `if org == "that one school", passing score = 80, else 60`. Every custom term a customer negotiated meant one more check like that, buried somewhere in the product.

**Behavior should differ across customers because of a value someone set, not because of a check someone wrote.**

This is part 5 of the series on our identity system. Part 4 covered the feature flag — the switch for a rollout in progress. This part draws the line to the other place per-customer differences live: a catalog of settings that outlast the rollout, for as long as the contract does.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Problem** | To give one customer a different threshold or feature, someone writes a check for that customer's identity, directly in the logic. |
| **Why** | There's no place to hold "this customer's value" separate from "this customer's identity," so the identity itself becomes the switch. |
| **Goal** | A customer's custom behavior comes from a value configured for them — the logic itself never has to change. |

## Why it matters

**Every one of those checks is the product promising to remember one customer, by name, forever, in that exact spot.** Multiply that by every customer who's ever negotiated a term, scattered across the product — nobody can answer "what's different about this customer" without hunting for every spot and hoping they found them all.

- **Miss one,** and the customer who's supposed to pass at 80 gets graded at 60 wherever it was missed.
- **Onboard the next customer,** and it means finding that logic again and adding one more check beside the last.

## Give the org a value, not an identity check

**The product asks "what's this customer's value for this setting," never "which customer is this."**

Org config replaces the identity check with a lookup:

- **A catalog declares what's configurable and its type** — passing score is a number, a feature is on or off, a currency is one of a fixed set.
- **Each customer gets their own value** for whatever applies to them; anyone who never negotiated a term gets the platform default.
- **The logic never mentions a specific customer** — it asks for this customer's current value and behaves accordingly, whoever they are.

## Keep the contract term out of the flag drawer

**A feature flag disappears once its rollout is done. A customer's contract term doesn't — it lives as long as the contract does.**

It's tempting to reach for the flag system, since it already lets you turn something on or off per customer. But a flag exists to be deleted — covered in the earlier post on shipping dark and releasing on purpose. A contract term exists to last. Put it behind a flag, and it either gets swept up in a cleanup meant for rollout scaffolding, or the flag system fills up with switches nobody dares delete.

**One rule:** temporary and on/off is a flag; permanent and per-customer is config.

## The trade-off

| | Check the customer's identity in the logic | Catalog + per-customer values |
|---|---|---|
| Onboarding a custom term | Find the logic, add a check, ship it | Set a value |
| "What's different about this customer?" | Search the product and hope you found it all | Read their configured values |
| Risk of missing a spot | High, one check per place the logic runs | None, one lookup everywhere |

The cost lands on engineering up front: a catalog has to exist before the first value is stored in it. It pays for itself by the third or fourth customer with a custom term — the alternative is a product riddled with identity checks only the person who wrote each one fully understands.

## What's next

The earlier post on shipping dark covers the flag side of this boundary. Together, the rule is: if it's part of what the product does for a customer for the life of their contract, it's config; if it's here to stage a release, it's a flag.
