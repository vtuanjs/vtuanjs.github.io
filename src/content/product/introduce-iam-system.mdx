A customer running their orders in one of our apps asked to also run their warehouse in another — same company, one login, and let the order system hand fulfilment straight to the warehouse. I scoped it expecting a small feature. Instead I found each app had its own login, its own users, its own idea of what a company was, and none of them could say these two accounts belonged to the same customer. The feature wasn't slow to build. It was impossible — the product had no place to record that these two accounts were one company.

**The fix is one system that owns identity — who a customer is, who belongs to them, and what each is allowed to do — that every app reads from instead of keeping its own copy.**

This is the first post in a series on building that system. This part frames the problem and introduces the five pieces; later parts go deep on each one.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Problem** | Each app keeps its own copy of who a customer is, what they can do, and how they're configured. A relationship that spans apps — one company across two of them, one login, one connection — has nowhere to live. |
| **Why** | Every app was shipped fast, on its own, owning everything it needed. That independence went straight into the data, and the business only outgrew it later — the first time two apps had to agree on who a customer is. |
| **Goal** | One system owns identity. Every app reads from it, so the answer is always the same — and relationships across apps become facts to record, not integrations to invent. |

## Why it matters

**The cost isn't a messier codebase. It's a class of product you can't sell:**

- **No single account.** You can't offer one login that works across every app, because no app can say two accounts are the same company.
- **No cross-app handoff.** You can't let one app feed another, because neither agrees on who the customer is.
- **No clean onboarding.** Onboarding a customer onto the full suite is three separate setups kept in step by hand.
- **Every cross-app feature hits the same wall** — the relationship it depends on has nowhere to live.

**It compounds with every app you add:**

- One more copy of the customer.
- One more answer that can disagree with the others.
- One more integration to hand-build the next time two apps need to know about the same company.

**Centralizing identity doesn't add complexity — it removes the hidden complexity that was already there,** spread across every app that kept its own copy.

### Organization — one customer record, everything else follows

**Benefit:** One action creates the customer. Every other system follows automatically.

**Why this exists:** signup touches billing, support, access control, analytics — every team needs to know the customer exists. Without one owner, each team provisions its own copy. One failed step leaves the customer half-created, and no one can tell where. Part 2.

  <figcaption>Every customer in one list — code, name, status, and which apps they're provisioned for. One record, one place to look.</figcaption>
</figure>

### User — one person, consistent access everywhere

**Benefit:** One authoritative answer to "who is this person and what are they allowed to do" — regardless of which part of the product is asking.

**Why this exists:** the same person can be an admin in their own account and read-only in a partner's. Without one system tracking that, access bugs follow — wrong people see things, right people get blocked, and there's no single place to fix it. Part 3.

### Feature flag — test with real customers before rolling out to everyone

**Benefit:** Faster, safer launches. Pilots with specific customers before committing to everyone. No emergency deploys to turn something off.

**Why this exists:** features used to ship to everyone at once or stay hidden until fully ready. A flag turns a feature on for one or two customers first, widens the rollout as it proves out, and turns it off in seconds if something breaks. Part 4.

<figure>
  <Image src={iamFeatureFlag} alt="Feature Flags list showing key, name, description, toggle status, mode, and organization count" />
  <figcaption>Every feature flag in one list — toggle on or off, set allowlist or denylist mode, and see exactly which organizations are included. No deploy needed.</figcaption>
</figure>

### Config — each customer's settings, in one governed place

**Benefit:** Each customer's configuration lives in one place the whole product reads consistently, with no guesswork about which version is current.

**Why this exists:** customers differ on pricing, currency, invoice format, SLA tier. Without a governed place for that, the differences scatter — spreadsheets, code exceptions, tribal knowledge — and drift into the wrong behavior for the wrong customer. Part 5.

<figure>
  <Image src={iamConfig} alt="Config detail page showing API_RATE_LIMIT with per-organization values for org-epsilon and org-alpha" />
  <figcaption>Each config key shows which organizations have a custom value and what it is. Different customers, different limits — all in one governed place.</figcaption>
</figure>

### Rules — business logic that anyone can change, without a deploy

**Benefit:** The right people can change business logic directly — validated, auditable, live without a deploy. Engineering is no longer the bottleneck for every rule change.

**Why this exists:** shipping fees, discount eligibility, pricing tiers change on a business schedule, not an engineering one. In code, every change needs a developer and a deploy. In a free-text box, one typo breaks checkout. Part 6.

## The trade-off: one shared system means one shared dependency

Centralizing these decisions is not free.

| | Each app owns its own identity | One shared system |
|---|---|---|
| "Are these accounts the same company?" | A different answer in each app, if any | One answer, everywhere |
| Selling the full suite | Three separate accounts to keep in step | One account across every app |
| Connecting one app to another | Hand-built integration each time | A relationship to record, not invent |
| Onboarding a new customer | Each app provisions separately — easy to miss one | One action, everything follows |
| Risk if the system is down | Isolated to one app | Broader impact |

**The real cost is concentration:** if this system breaks, more of the product feels it. That's real, and it's managed deliberately — not ignored.

The per-app model doesn't avoid the cost. It hides it as features you can't ship and integrations you rebuild by hand, one cross-app request at a time. A single owner turns the answer into a fact instead of a guess.
