We have 3PL operators using our platform — one person, one login, working across several client warehouses. In one client's organization they're a full operator: adjust stock, close orders, manage the warehouse. In another, they're view-only — check a shipment, see a status, nothing else. Same login, same day, two different jobs depending on whose warehouse they're standing in. The first time this came up, the honest answer to "what can this person do?" was: it depends which client you're asking about. Our access model had no place to hold two different answers for one person.

**A person's access should never be one fixed role — it should be computed fresh for whichever organization they're currently working in.**

This is part 3 of the series on our identity system. Part 1 introduced the five pieces; part 2 covered how a new organization provisions itself. This part is the second piece, the user: one person, and what "what can they do" actually means once that person belongs to more than one organization.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Problem** | The same person can be a full operator in one client's organization and view-only in another. A single role on the account has nowhere to hold both. |
| **Why** | Access was designed for the first customer, who belonged to exactly one organization. It only breaks once a person legitimately belongs to two. |
| **Goal** | Every person gets two independent answers — a company-wide standing, and a role per organization — computed for whichever organization the request is actually about. |

## Why it matters

**If access is one role on the account, the first person who legitimately needs different permissions in two organizations breaks the model.** Teams patch around it:

- A note in a spreadsheet.
- A manual override.
- A support ticket every time someone's access needs to differ by client.

None of that scales past a handful of exceptions. Every one is a support burden and a security risk — someone with more access than they should have, in an organization they shouldn't have touched.

**It also blocks a whole category of customer:** 3PL operators, agencies, partner staff — people who work across multiple client organizations at a different trust level in each. If the platform can't express "operator here, viewer there" for one login, those customers either don't fit the product, or get a workaround that quietly under- or over-shares access until someone notices.

## Two questions, not one

**"What can this person do" is actually two separate questions, and they don't have the same answer:**

- **Company-wide standing — "who is this person to us?"** Our employee, our support agent, or an outside customer. Fixed for that person, everywhere, regardless of which client's warehouse they're currently in.
- **Per-organization role — "what can they do in this specific warehouse?"** Full operator, manager, view-only, or not a member at all. Meaningless on its own — it only means something once you name the organization.

Collapsing these into one role forces one question to answer for the other: either day-to-day trust in a specific warehouse gets flattened to match the account-level role, or the account-level role gets stretched to cover per-client differences it was never meant to hold. Both produce access that's wrong somewhere.

## The organization membership is where per-client truth lives

**The moment a person joins a client's organization, that relationship needs its own record** — not a flag bolted onto the person, not a flag bolted onto the client. The membership holds the real, changeable facts:

- Which role they have in this specific organization.
- Whether they've been disabled from it.
- Whether they're the main point of contact for it.

A person can be an active, full operator in one client's organization and a disabled, view-only member in another, at the same moment — nothing about one membership leaks into how the other is read.

This also separates two flows people conflate: adding an existing platform user to a new client organization, versus inviting someone who has never used the platform at all. Both end with a membership record. Only one starts with creating the person in the first place.

## Compute access for one organization at a time

**The system should only ever compute access for the one organization a request is actually about — never all of a person's organizations at once.** The moment someone switches clients, the platform recomputes their permissions for that organization alone. Access to every other organization they belong to sits untouched, unread, in the background.

**This isn't just tidier — it caps the damage of the worst case.** A compromised session or credential can only act as far as the one organization it was scoped to, not every client organization the account belongs to. And because recomputing access for the active organization is cheap, switching clients costs nothing.

## The trade-off

| | One role per account | Two axes, scoped per organization |
|---|---|---|
| Same person, different access per client | Not representable | The normal case |
| "What can they do?" | A guess, unless you already know which client | Always answered relative to one organization |
| Per-client facts (disabled, main contact) | Nowhere to record them | Live on the membership |
| Damage if access is compromised | Every organization the account touches | Only the one organization in view |
| Switching which client you're working in | n/a | Access recomputed for that client, instantly |
| What it costs to build | Nothing extra | A membership record per person-per-org, and an explicit "which organization is this request about" on every check |

**The cost is genuine plumbing:** a membership record per person-organization pair, and every access check has to know which organization it's about before it can answer. That's more to build than one role on an account. But the one-role model doesn't avoid the cost — it defers it to the first partner, agency, or 3PL operator who needs different access in different clients, where it shows up as a support ticket and a manual workaround instead of a designed feature.

**One honest limit:** disabling someone from an organization doesn't cut off a permission already computed and handed to them mid-session — it takes effect on the next recompute, which is fast but not instant. Anything urgent enough that instant matters needs a separate, explicit "cut this person off right now" action, not the normal refresh cycle.
