A customer running its orders in one app asked to also run its warehouse in another — same company, one login, and let the order system hand fulfilment straight to the warehouse. A simple ask. I went to scope it and found each app had its own login, its own users, its own idea of what an organization was, and nothing in any of them could say these two accounts were the same company. The feature wasn't slow to build. It was impossible to build.

**Identity built separately for each app can't represent the relationships the business grows into. When the business needs one of those relationships, no amount of engineering time gets you there — the model has no place to put it.**

## What consolidating unlocks

The return isn't a cleaner diagram — it's a class of product the old structure couldn't ship. The business can sell one account that works across every app. Partners can wire one app's output into another's input. Onboarding a customer becomes one action instead of three that have to be kept in step. The team stops being blocked from building cross-app features at all, because the relationships those features depend on finally have a place to live.

## Why wiring the apps together doesn't hold

**My instinct was to build the connection; but what the business was asking for wasn't a connection between apps, it was a fact about identity — and no app owned identity.** When orders needed to reach the warehouse, point-to-point integration worked for that one link. It stopped working the moment the requirement became "one company owns all three apps" or "one user belongs to two orgs." Those are properties of a customer that every app has to agree on, and I couldn't message my way to agreement that had no source.

So each integration was re-implementing "is this the same customer?" against three different answers, and every app I added multiplied the glue. I didn't have an integration problem. I had an identity that lived in the wrong number of places.

## The real constraint: the org shape got baked into the data

**The constraint was never technical difficulty. It was that a decision I made for delivery speed silently became the shape of the product.** I built three apps because I shipped three problems independently, and the fastest path was to let each own everything it needed — users and organizations included. That independence went straight into the data model. When the business model stopped being independent — customers who wanted the whole suite, partners who wanted the apps to feed each other — the data was still shaped like three separate businesses.

I never chose "our customers can never span apps." It got chosen for me, by the shortcut, and it stayed invisible until the first request that needed the apps to know each other.

## The call: pull identity into one owned system — on the first relationship, not the third

I built one system to own identity: who a customer is, which organizations exist, who belongs to which, and everything that gates what they can do. Every app reads from it; none keep their own copy.

The timing mattered as much as the decision. The cost of moving identity only grows — every app you add and every user record that accumulates makes the migration heavier. The signal to consolidate is the *first* business request that needs two apps to agree on who the customer is, not the third — I learned that by nearly missing it, rebuilding live under a deadline I'd already blown past. When the first cross-app relationship lands, that's the moment identity earns its own owner.

The deep tour of each piece — what the records are, why they exist, and what breaks without them — is a separate write-up: [Identity and access management as one system](/product/introduce-iam-system).

## What changed my mind

I used to read "keep it simple" as "let each app own its own users." Fewer dependencies, no central team to coordinate with, every app shipping on its own clock. For three independent apps that was correct, and I'd make the same call again at that stage. What I got wrong was treating it as a permanent architecture instead of a stage.

The belief broke the day a routine ask — let one company use two of our apps with one login — turned into a multi-month rebuild. The ask needed a property of an identity model we'd never built. The shortcut hadn't made us slower; it had built a wall, and we only saw it when we walked into it.

## The trade-off

A single owned identity system trades many small, invisible costs for one large, visible one. I think that's usually the right trade — a cost you can see is a cost you can manage — but it's real, and it fails in specific ways.

| Benefit | Cost | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Relationships across apps become expressible | Every app now depends on one team | The owning team becomes a bottleneck; every identity change queues behind it |
| No app re-implements "who is this customer" | A genuine single point of failure | The system goes down and every app degrades at once |
| One customer, one account, one onboarding | The owning team must run like infrastructure | Staffed as a feature team, it falls behind, apps quietly keep their own copies again — recreating the fragmentation you paid to remove |

The bottleneck bit first for me. The moment one system owns identity, every app's change to a user, an org, or a permission goes through the team that owns it — and a team funded like a feature team falls behind, at which point apps route around it and the copies come back. My fix wasn't to hand ownership back; that just returns the wall. It was to run the owning team as infrastructure: capacity ahead of demand, self-service for the common changes, and a clear line between what genuinely needs the owning team and what an app team can safely do itself. Centralize what identity *is*; push the work of changing it out to the edges.

## One decision for tomorrow

Next time a product or business request needs two of your apps to know about the same customer, org, or user, stop before you scope the integration and ask one thing: is this a connection between two apps, or a relationship your identity model can't express? If it's the second, you've found the wall — and the cheapest time to move identity into one owner is that first request, not the third.
